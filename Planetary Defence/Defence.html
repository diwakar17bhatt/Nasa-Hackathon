Planetary Defence

Planetary defense against Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) has become an increasingly important focus for space agencies and researchers worldwide. NEOs include asteroids and comets that come within 1.3 astronomical units of the Earth and pose potential threats due to their size and impact energy. Here are the main strategies and technologies currently being developed and employed for planetary defense:

Detection and Tracking


Telescopes and Surveys:

Ground-based and space-based telescopes, like the Pan-STARRS and NEOWISE missions, are crucial for detecting NEOs. They help track their orbits and predict future paths. Surveys aim to identify as many NEOs as possible, especially those larger than 140 meters, which could cause significant damage upon impact.

Data Sharing:

Collaborative efforts among international space agencies, such as NASA, ESA, and others, enhance the detection capabilities and provide a global tracking network.

Characterization

Once detected, understanding the physical properties of NEOs is essential:

Spectroscopy

This technique analyzes the light reflected from NEOs to determine their composition, size, and structure. Radar Imaging: Radar data can provide detailed information about an object's shape, rotation, and surface characteristics, aiding in assessing potential impact risks.

Impact Mitigation Strategies

If a potential impact is identified, several strategies are considered:


Kinetic Impactor:

Gravity Tractor:

A spacecraft could hover near the NEO, using its gravitational pull to gradually alter the object's path over time. This method requires precise calculations and long lead times.


Nuclear Detonation:

As a last resort, a nuclear device could be detonated near the NEO to change its trajectory. This method raises concerns about fallout and is still highly theoretical.
